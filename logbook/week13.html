From the total of false negative metric, barbell dorado, and fastplong might seem to damage the SNP variant calling perfomrance if we compare them against the untrimmed dataste across three different depths in sup datasets. In low depth samples, untrimmed dataset perfomed best with fewer false negative SNp calls. By trimming adapter sequence (that is defined internally by each tool), it might also remove useful data that is importat for the variant caller?

variant callers and assemblers are robust: they are already “smart” enough to distinguish artificial sequences (like adapters or barcodes) from true biological sequences. They also inherently know how to prioritise and utilize high quality reads during the assembly or calling process. Also they might do not see good quality data only based on phred score.

Trimming might causes data loss: Trimming can harm the analysis through over-trimming. A tool might unnecessarily remove 2-5 bp of actual sample sequence from thousands of reads across the dataset, resulting in data loss that potentially will be usefull. Depending on the algorithm, it may also falsely identify true biological sequences as adapters (false positives) and trim them out. A variant caller or an assembler may outsmart the QC tools.

Filtering might discrad useful information: In the case of quality filtering, we might discard “bad” reads based on strict length or quality score thresholds. However, these reads might not actually be bad enough to negatively impact modern variant callers or genome assemblers. Strict filtering causes us to lose potentially useful data and structural information.

import itertools

trimming_tools = ["porechop_abi_split", "fastplong", "barbell", "dorado", "untrimmed"]

# all possible combinations
